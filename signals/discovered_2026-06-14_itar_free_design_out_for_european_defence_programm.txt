Since 2024 the EU's strategic-autonomy push (EDIP, the €1.5bn European Defence Industry Programme adopted 2025, and France/Germany's 'buy-European' procurement language) has made US ITAR/EAR export-controlled content a procurement liability rather than a convenience: a single State-Department-controlled part can give Washington a veto over re-export and sale of a whole European weapon system. European primes and their tier-2 suppliers are now newly penalised for ITAR-tainted bills of materials, yet most have no systematic map of where US-controlled components hide in their supply chains. The opening is adversarial across jurisdictions — engineering US export-control exposure OUT of a design to defeat a foreign regulator's reach — which neither the US nor the EU will ever build for them, and the value compounds as 'sovereign' content rules tighten.
