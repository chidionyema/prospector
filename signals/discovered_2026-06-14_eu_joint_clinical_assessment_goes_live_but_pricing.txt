Under EU HTA Regulation 2021/2282, the Joint Clinical Assessment (JCA) became mandatory in January 2025 for new oncology drugs and advanced-therapy medicinal products, forcing manufacturers to submit one centralised clinical-evidence dossier on a fixed EU timeline. But the JCA report is explicitly non-binding: each member state still runs its own pricing, reimbursement and relative-effectiveness appraisal, often reaching opposite conclusions from the same dossier. Pharma and biotech sponsors are now squeezed between a single high-stakes EU evidence submission and 27 diverging national appraisals that the EU body legally cannot harmonise. The opening is to defend and optimise the sponsor's evidence position across that gap — work the regulator structurally will never do.
